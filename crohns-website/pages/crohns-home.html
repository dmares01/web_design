What Exactly is Crohns?

Hover over image to enlarge

To put it simply, Crohn's is an autoimmune disease. Well that just leads to more questions. What is an autoimmune disease? What does that mean? Dont worry I will try my best to explain all of that in more, hopefully in aome way you can understand unlike most of the medical websites out there right now. A lot of these questions were the exact same ones I had when I was first diagnosed with crohn's disease.

Please use the links above to find out more about symptoms, what body parts are affected by crohn's, and if you're feeling brave we can get into the really nitty gritty details.


Common Symptoms

  • Frequent, recurring diarrhea
  • Mouth sores
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Fever
  • Abdominal pain and cramping
  • Fatigue and a feeling of low energy
  • Reduced appetite

More Severe Symptoms

  • Rectal Bleeding
  • Blood in stool
  • Fistulas (Drainage tubes to remove puss)
  • Inflammation of skin, eyes, and joints
  • Inflammation of liver or bile ducts
  • Delayed growth in children

Diarrhea

Definition: A condition in which feces are discharged from the bowels frequently and in a liquid form

Although Diarrhea happens to everyone, unfortunately it is quite common in people with Crohn's disease. Your digestive tract can be pretty finicky at times and Diarrhea is especially common during a flare up. Many states will offer a bathroom card allowing you to use private restrooms or skip a line if you need too. I've had my card for nine years and have not had to use it yet, but it is still something I carry around with me just in case.

Mouth Sores

Definition: A small ulcer of the mouth or lips.

Mouth sores are one of those things were they are an inconvenience but do not hinder your day to day life unless unchecked. Most will go away on there own in about a week, although it will vary from person to person.

Unexplained Weight Loss

Definition: A sudden loss in weight not explainable by diet, exercise, or other means

When you have Crohn's disease and parts of your digestive tract are inflamed (common during a flare up), your ability to absorb vitamins and nutrients can be severely impacted. This inability to absorb nutrients can cause malnutrition, or simply prevent you from getting enough calories each day.

Fever

Definition: an abnormally high body temperature, usually accompanied by shivering, headache, and in severe instances, delirium

What else is there to say? A fever is a fever and everyone is going to get them. Personally I feel that a fever could be a symptom for almost anything in the world. Although with the other complications that can be present in Crohn's disease, having a fever can become more common.

Abdominal Pain and Cramping

Definition: Abdominal pain can be acute or chronic. Can refer to pain that is perceived anywhere in the abdominal area.

I'll be real, abdominal pain is one of those things that will be very common. There will be days that it's just annoying and can be ignored, and then there are those days you don't even want to get out of bed. No matter how mild or severe your Crohn's is, you will experience abdominal pain at some point.

Fatigue

Definition: extreme tiredness resulting from mental or physical exertion or illness

This is another one that is quite common. When your body is having a flare up it's going to take a lot out of you. Your energy levels will not be the same as when you're in remission.

Reduced Appetite

Definition: A reduced desire to eat. May also be known as a poor appetite or loss of appetite

When your stomach hurts or is upset, you're just not gonna want to eat. Although food will oftentimes help, sometimes that's easier said than done. Bland soup and crackers will be your be your best friend.

Rectal Bleeding

Definition: Refers to the passage of bright blood (often mixed with clots or stools) via the rectum

This one is a big red flag (no pun intended). Any bleeding should be brought up to the doctor as soon as possible. This means that a decent amount of inflammation is going on inside your intestines and needs to get looked at. If left unchecked it can end up doing more damage to your system and worsen your Crohn's.

Blood in Stool

Definition: This refers to bleeding somewhere in your digestive tract. Sometimes the amount of blood is so small that it can only be detected by a fecal occult test

Very similar and will often go hand in hand with the rectal bleeding. This occurs when you're bleeding but in smaller amounts. AS your stool passes through your system the blood will come out with it.

Fistulas

Definition: an abnormal or surgically made passage between a hollow or tubular organ and the body surface, or between two hollow or tubular organs.

These absolutely suck. They can be very painful, they're drainage tubes for pus so they aren't very pretty, and they can just be uncomfortable. Fistula's can close up and heal when your Crohn's is in remission.

Inflammation of Skin, Eyes, and Joints

Definition: Inflammation is the body's attempt at self-protection to remove harmful stimuli and begin the healing process

For this, inflammation of the joints is the most common. Sore joints can be reoccurring, obviously it varies with the person. Having your eyes be bloodshot can also happen often depending on how well your Crohn's is controlled.

Inflammation of Liver or Bile Ducts

Definition: A reaction that occurs when liver cells are attacked by a disease-causing microbe or substance

I can't say I have any personal experience with this one but from talking with people in the community or doctors, it doesn't seem to be very prevalent. When it does occur it is most often detected by tests done by a doctor.

Delayed Growth in Children

Definition: When a child isn't growing at the normal rate for their age

Honestly I feel that this definition is redundant. It's true that kids diagnosed with Crohn's disease can have stunted growth. Oftentimes doctors will correct this right away, or do their best to ensure that the child is still able to continue their growth.


What is a Flare Up?

Hover over image to enlarge

A flare up is when your Crohn's disease is having problems. This is often signified by an increase in inflammation in the affected areas of the body, increase in fatigue and exhaustion, as well as worsening of the other symptoms. The increase in inflammation is often the biggest issue. Everyone's body uses inflammation to fight off bacteria or viruses. It is the part of the body's self defense mechanism. Having Crohn's disease your body will inflame areas larger than it should, and stay inflamed longer than it should. This can cause further damage to your intestines and end up making things worse.

You and your doctor will try and keep your body out of a flare up as much as possible. It will take some experimenting to figure out what works best for you and what is needed, but it will get figured out. As you and your doctor figure out the best plan, than your body should start to enter remission.


Remission: The Place to be

Hover over image to enlarge

Remission is where you want to be. It means that your Crohn's is under control and in good shape. Once you have a plan that works, all it requires is sticking to it. You may need small tweaks here or there, but once your body is in remission it is much easier to stay there.

I like to explain it similar to a pendulum. When your body is in a flare up you are all the way at one end of the pendulum. In order to balance your body back out you must counter with stronger medicines, more exercise, and a bette diet. This will start to pull your body back towards the middle. As you start to get closer to the middle you can slowly loosen up your diet and exercise routines. The maintenance required to stay in remission is a lot less than the effort required to get there. As they say an object in motion stays in motion.


What Body Parts are Affected?

Any part of the Gastrointenstinal Tract (GI Tract) can be affected by Crohn's disease. The GI Tract is just a fancy name for the Digestive Tract. This encompasses the mouth and esophagus all the way to the anus. Different parts of the GI tract affected each have their own name.

Ileocolitis

Affects the end of the small intestine (the ileum) and the large intestine (the colon).

Ileitis

Affects the end of the small intestine (the ileum).

Jejunoileitis

Characterized by patchy areas of inflammation in the upper half of the small intestine (the jejunum).

Gastroduodenal Crohn’s disease

Affects the stomach and the beginning of the small intestine (the duodenum).

Crohn’s colitis

Affects the large intestine (the colon).
